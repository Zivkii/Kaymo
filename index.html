A Regional Crisis: Somalia's Outlier Rate

Somalia's deforestation rate of 1.03% annually is alarmingly out of sync with its neighbors and the rest of the continent. It's nearly double the African average and triple that of neighboring Kenya, highlighting the uniquely severe pressures on Somalia's forests.

The Primary Driver: The Illicit Charcoal Trade

The Cycle of Destruction

The illegal charcoal trade is a highly organized, destructive industry. It begins with the felling of native trees, funds conflict, and ends with environmental ruin. This lucrative trade persists despite UN and government bans, revealing a deep-rooted governance crisis.

1

Ancient Acacia trees are illegally felled, destroying vital ecosystems.

2

The wood is converted to charcoal in inefficient kilns, releasing harmful emissions.

3

Criminal groups and militias tax the trade, earning millions annually to fuel conflict.

4

Charcoal is illegally exported, primarily to Gulf States, for huge profits.

Charcoal production is the single largest driver of deforestation, dwarfing all other factors combined.
